father of gravity

Sir Isaac Newton (1642–1727), often called the "father of gravity," was an English physicist and mathematician whose work revolutionized science. He is best known for formulating the law of universal gravitation, which explained that every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Beyond gravity, Newton also developed the three laws of motion, laid the foundations of calculus, and made significant contributions to optics. His most famous work, Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica (1687), unified celestial and terrestrial mechanics under the same principles, showing that the force pulling an apple to the ground is the same force that governs planetary motion.
